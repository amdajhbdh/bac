# Reaction Rates

## Definition
The rate of a chemical reaction is the change in concentration of reactants or products per unit time.

**Formula**: Rate = -Δ[Reactant]/Δt = Δ[Product]/Δt

Units: mol/(L·s) or M/s

## Rate Expression
For reaction: aA + bB → cC + dD

Rate = -(1/a)(Δ[A]/Δt) = -(1/b)(Δ[B]/Δt) = (1/c)(Δ[C]/Δt) = (1/d)(Δ[D]/Δt)

## Factors Affecting Rate

### 1. Concentration
Higher concentration → More collisions → Faster rate

### 2. Temperature
Higher temperature → More kinetic energy → Faster rate

**Rule of thumb**: Rate doubles for every 10°C increase

### 3. Surface Area
Larger surface area → More collisions → Faster rate

### 4. Catalysts
Lower activation energy → Faster rate
- Not consumed in reaction
- Provide alternative pathway

### 5. Nature of Reactants
- Physical state (gas > liquid > solid)
- Bond strength
- Molecular complexity

## Rate Law
Rate = k[A]ᵐ[B]ⁿ

Where:
- k = rate constant
- m, n = reaction orders (determined experimentally)
- Overall order = m + n

## Reaction Orders

### Zero Order
Rate = k (independent of concentration)
[A] vs time: linear

### First Order
Rate = k[A]
[A] vs time: exponential decay
Half-life: t₁/₂ = 0.693/k

### Second Order
Rate = k[A]^2
1/[A] vs time: linear

## Integrated Rate Laws

| Order | Integrated Form | Linear Plot |
|-------|----------------|-------------|
| 0 | [A] = -kt + [A]₀ | [A] vs t |
| 1 | ln[A] = -kt + ln[A]₀ | ln[A] vs t |
| 2 | 1/[A] = kt + 1/[A]₀ | 1/[A] vs t |

## Half-Life
Time for concentration to decrease to half its initial value

- Zero order: t₁/₂ = [A]₀/(2k)
- First order: t₁/₂ = 0.693/k
- Second order: t₁/₂ = 1/(k[A]₀)

## Examples

**Example 1**: [A] decreases from 1.0 M to 0.5 M in 10 s
Average rate = (1.0-0.5)/10 = 0.05 M/s

**Example 2**: First order, k = 0.1 s^{-1}
t₁/₂ = 0.693/0.1 = 6.93 s
